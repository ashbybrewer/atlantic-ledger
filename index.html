01 / THE SCALE

The archive is not the trade.

SlaveVoyages reconstructs tens of thousands of crossings from shipping records, port books, court cases, newspapers, and scholarship. Even this immense archive is incomplete.

Visual ruleOn the aggregate map, one moving dot represents 25 people recorded on a voyage. It does not pretend to be a named person.
02 / BEFORE THE COAST

No single cause explains enslavement.

People entered the trade through warfare and raiding, judicial punishment, kidnapping, debt and pawning, tribute, and political upheaval. African brokers, rulers, merchants, and captors operated inside systems transformed and enlarged by Atlantic demand.

warfareraidingjudicial salekidnappingdebtpolitical upheaval

These are documented system-level drivers. They are not assigned to a person unless a source says so.

03 / A SHIP BECOMES LEGIBLE

The Zong, 1781.

A surviving voyage record can name the ship, its route, dates, people involved in the venture, and counts. It still cannot restore the identities of the people held aboard.

04 / WHEN A NAME SURVIVES

Bora, recorded in 1819.

The record preserves a name, age, sex, height, ship, embarkation port, captain, court outcome, and later fate. It does not preserve Bora’s ancestral home or cause of enslavement.

05 / THE ILLEGAL ERA

The Clotilda, 1860.

More than half a century after the United States prohibited trans-Atlantic importation, the schooner sailed from Mobile to Ouidah and returned with 110 people recorded as embarked. Kossola lets us draw a rare named-person route beyond the ship.

06 / AFTER LANDING

The voyage record ends. A life does not.

Auctions, sales, forced relocations, plantations, births, marriages, resistance, manumissions, and deaths must be joined from other archives. This is where Enslaved.org and the Slave Societies Digital Archive enter the build.

shipauctionresidencefamilyfate

A connection appears only when shared names, dates, places, relationships, or scholarly linkage support it.

METHOD / EVIDENCE BEFORE EFFECT

Precision must include the precision of uncertainty.

DOCUMENTED

A record directly states it.

Ship names, dates, ports, recorded names, sale events, relationships, and archival citations.

INFERRED

Evidence supports a bounded reconstruction.

Imputed voyage counts, likely language groups, regional origins, and scholarly record linkages.

UNKNOWN

The archive does not permit the claim.

Ancestral village, cause of capture, auction, plantation, or fate when no joined source survives.

BIOARCHAEOLOGY

Corroboration, not ethnic labeling.

Ancient DNA and isotope evidence can narrow possible origins in particular studies. It cannot responsibly assign every person to a modern ethnic group.
